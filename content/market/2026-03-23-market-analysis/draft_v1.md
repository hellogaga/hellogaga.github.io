## Executive Summary

The Swedish electricity market in SE3 on March 23, 2026, experienced a notable rebound in day-ahead spot prices compared to the previous day, though remaining below the 7-day average. The average spot price settled at 383.54 SEK/MWh, influenced by a volatile hourly profile with significant peaks during morning and evening demand. Price spreads across bidding areas remained substantial, with SE3 trading at a premium to northern zones but at a discount to SE4, indicating persistent transmission limitations. Ancillary service markets, particularly mFRR EAM, showed strong opportunities for upward regulation, driven by system imbalances. Geopolitical tensions surrounding the Iran conflict continued to exert upward pressure on global energy markets, indirectly impacting sentiment.

## News & Geopolitical Context

The geopolitical landscape, particularly the escalating Iran conflict, dominated global headlines and cast a long shadow over energy markets. President Donald Trump's threats over the weekend to attack Iranian energy infrastructure if the Strait of Hormuz was not reopened within 48 hours, followed by Iran's counter-threats to mine the Persian Gulf, caused immediate market anxiety. The oil price (Brent) surged to approximately 114 dollars per barrel at the opening of international trading on March 23, up from 112.19 dollars per barrel. This initial market reaction, described as "waiting for a black Monday on the stock exchange," underscored the sensitivity to supply chain disruptions and energy security concerns.

However, later in the day, Trump announced a temporary five-day pause in attacks on Iranian energy targets, leading to a reversal in stock market trends and some stabilization. Despite this temporary de-escalation, the underlying tensions remain high, with Saudi Arabia warning that its patience is "not endless." The conflict's potential to disrupt global oil and gas transports (one-fifth of which pass through Hormuz) continues to fuel uncertainty and drive up fuel and insurance prices, indirectly influencing electricity market sentiment and generation costs, especially for thermal power.

Domestically, the Swedish government announced measures to mitigate the economic impact of the global crisis, including reduced taxes on fuel and strengthened electricity support. These policies aim to ease the burden on households but do not directly address the structural issues of power supply and transmission. Of particular note for the Swedish power system is the application by Kärnfull Next to build 4-6 small modular reactors (SMRs) near Valdemarsvik, signaling a long-term strategic shift towards nuclear power to enhance energy security and reduce price volatility. This development, if realized, could significantly alter the energy mix in southern Sweden.

Several market announcements (UMMs) also affected the Nordic region:
*   A planned unavailability of transmission capacity from SE3 to NO1, SE2 to NO3, NO5 to NO3, and NO3 to NO5 was published at 21:48 CET, indicating foreseen maintenance by Statnett SF. This could impact cross-border flows and regional prices.
*   An "Other market information" update from Svenska kraftnät at 18:50 CET confirmed that the ATC (Available Transfer Capacity) for the FI->SE3 border would be zero for the mFRR market, effective from March 18, 16:00 CET to March 23, 18:45 CET. This is a critical factor for mFRR market participants, as it restricts Finnish imports for balancing purposes in SE3.
*   Planned maintenance at Rya KVV (SE3) by Göteborg Energi AB, scheduled from March 23, 14:49 CET, for "Modification of burners and Tube," represents a local reduction in generation capacity within SE3.
*   Svenska kraftnät also announced planned transmission unavailability between SE4 and LT, and SE3 and SE4, starting March 23, 11:10 CET, due to foreseen maintenance. This further exacerbates the already strained transmission capacity between SE3 and SE4, contributing to the high price differential.
*   An "Other market information" from Vattenfall AB published at 14:36 CET indicated an event affecting SE3 from March 23, 10:00 CET to March 26, 14:00 CET. The details of the unit name were not provided, but any Vattenfall-related event in SE3 could influence local supply.

These grid events, coupled with the broader geopolitical instability, underscore the complex interplay of factors influencing the Nordic electricity market.

## Implications for BESS in SE3

For Battery Energy Storage System (BESS) operators in SE3, today's market conditions presented clear opportunities, particularly in the ancillary services market. The high volatility in day-ahead spot prices, with significant peaks at 07:00 CET (613.59 SEK/MWh) and 18:00 CET (608.35 SEK/MWh) and a low at 02:00 CET (41.64 SEK/MWh), indicates strong potential for arbitrage strategies. BESS could profit by charging during off-peak hours (e.g., 01:00-03:00 CET) and discharging during peak demand periods.

However, the most compelling opportunities appear to be in the mFRR EAM market. The substantial average up-regulation spread of 73.76 EUR/MWh and the frequent activation of up-regulation (19 hours, 4219.0 MW total) signal a high value for providing upward flexibility. BESS units are well-suited to quickly respond to these signals, offering significant revenue streams. The negative average spread for down-regulation suggests that BESS should prioritize up-regulation services. The lifting of the zero ATC for FI->SE3 in the mFRR market, while not immediately impacting today's observed prices, could introduce more competition from imports in the future, potentially moderating mFRR up-regulation prices.

Given the persistent price differences between SE3 and SE4, BESS located strategically to alleviate congestion or participate in cross-border balancing, if feasible, could also unlock additional value. The planned maintenance at Rya KVV in SE3 further underscores the need for local flexible resources, potentially increasing the value of BESS in the coming days.

## Outlook

The outlook for the Nordic electricity market, particularly for SE3, remains complex and highly sensitive to both local grid conditions and global geopolitical developments. While the immediate de-escalation of the Iran conflict provided some relief, the underlying tensions and their potential impact on global energy prices will continue to be a dominant factor. Locally, persistent transmission constraints between Swedish bidding zones are expected to maintain price differentials, especially between SE3 and SE4. The long-term trend towards nuclear power, as indicated by the SMR application, suggests a future shift in the generation mix. For the near term, BESS operators should continue to monitor mFRR EAM for lucrative up-regulation opportunities and adapt to evolving grid events and cross-border capacity changes.
